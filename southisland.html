The South Island

Milford Sounds


Often described as the "eighth wonder of the world," Milford Sound is a breathtaking fjord on New Zealand’s South Island. Carved by ancient glaciers, it features towering granite peaks like Mitre Peak that rise dramatically from dark, deep waters. Despite its name, it is geographically a fjord, not a sound. The area is famous for its permanent waterfalls, like Stirling Falls, and the hundreds of temporary cascades that appear during heavy rainfall. It’s a thriving ecosystem home to fur seals, penguins, and bottlenose dolphins.

Malborough Sounds


The Marlborough Sounds sit at the tip of New Zealand’s South Island, a sprawling network of ancient sunken river valleys. Unlike the vertical cliffs of Milford, these sounds offer a gentler landscape of turquoise bays, lush native bush, and hidden coves. Comprised of Queen Charlotte, Kenepuru, and Pelorus Sounds, the region is a haven for boating and hiking, notably the Queen Charlotte Track. It is world-renowned for its Green-lipped mussels and premium Sauvignon Blanc, while providing a sanctuary for rare wildlife like the King Shag and Little Blue Penguin.

Mt Cook Nationl Park


Aoraki/Mount Cook National Park is the rugged heart of the Southern Alps, home to New Zealand’s highest peak (3,724m) and its longest glacier, the Haupapa/Tasman Glacier. Glaciers cover 40% of the park, carving dramatic U-shaped valleys and feeding iridescent blue lakes like Lake Pukaki. The park is a world-class destination for mountaineers and hikers, with the Hooker Valley Track offering accessible views of icebergs and granite faces. It also forms part of an International Dark Sky Reserve, offering some of the clearest stargazing on Earth.

Wanaka/Queenstown


Queenstown and Wānaka are the twin jewels of the Southern Lakes. Queenstown, the "Adventure Capital of the World," is a high-energy hub famous for bungy jumping and jet boating on Lake Wakatipu, set against the jagged Remarkables range. Nearby, Wānaka offers a more tranquil, laid-back alternative. It's the home Mount Aspiring National Park, it is home to the famous Wanaka Tree and legendary hikes like Roys Peak. While Queenstown thrives on adrenaline and vibrant nightlife, Wānaka excels in serene lake views and a boutique charm, together forming the best New Zealand alpine experience.

Kaikoura


Kaikōura is a breathtaking coastal town on New Zealand’s South Island, where the Seaward Kaikōura Range meets the Pacific Ocean. It is world-renowned as a premier destination for marine life encounters, thanks to the deep underwater canyon nearby. Visitors flock here to witness sperm whales, dusky dolphins, and fur seals in their natural habitat. Beyond the wildlife, it’s a haven for hikers and seafood lovers—the name itself literally means "meal of crayfish."

Franz Josef/Fox Glacier


Nestled within the Westland Tai Poutini National Park, these two "rivers of ice" are unique for plunging from the Southern Alps into lush, temperate rainforests. Just 25 minutes apart, they offer a dramatic landscape where ancient ice meets emerald greenery. Visitors can explore the glacier valleys on foot or take a heli-hike to walk directly on the blue ice. Nearby, the famous Lake Matheson offers iconic mirror reflections of New Zealand’s highest peaks, Aoraki/Mount Cook and Mount Tasman.

Abel Tasman National Park


Located at the top of the South Island, Abel Tasman is New Zealand’s smallest but most vibrant national park. It is a sun-drenched sanctuary of golden sand beaches, crystal-clear turquoise waters, and lush native bush. The park is best explored via the Abel Tasman Coast Track, a world-famous "Great Walk," or by kayaking along the rugged coastline to spot lounging fur seals. With no road access to its heart, the park remains a tranquil, pristine escape for hikers and sailors alike.

The Catlins


Located on the southeastern corner of the South Island, The Catlins is a rugged, off-the-beaten-path wilderness where the forest meets the sea. This region is famous for its dramatic waterfalls, such as Purakaunui Falls, and its ancient 180-million-year-old petrified forest at Curio Bay. It’s a sanctuary for rare wildlife; you’ll often find yellow-eyed penguins (Hoiho), Hector’s dolphins, and massive sea lions lounging on windswept beaches like Cannibal Bay.
