The North Island

Cathedral Cove


Located on New Zealand's Coromandel Peninsula, Cathedral Cove is a breathtaking coastal paradise accessible only by foot or boat. Its centerpiece is a massive, naturally formed limestone archway that connects two secluded white-sand beaches. Famed for its turquoise waters and dramatic rock formations like Te Hoho, it offers world-class snorkeling and photography. This iconic Marine Reserve perfectly captures the rugged, pristine beauty of the Pacific coastline.

Rotorua


Rotorua is New Zealand’s geothermal wonderland, famous for its dramatic volcanic activity and rich Māori culture. The air is often filled with a distinct sulfur scent, a reminder of the bubbling mud pools, steaming vents, and powerful geysers like Pōhutu that define the landscape. Beyond the thermal parks, it offers stunning lakeside views, the serene Redwoods Forest, and world-class mountain biking, making it a unique blend of natural power and adventure.

Rangitoto Island


Rangitoto Island is Auckland’s youngest and most iconic natural landmark. This dormant volcanic cone rises symmetrically from the Hauraki Gulf, featuring rugged lava fields and the world’s largest pōhutukawa forest. A hike to the summit offers panoramic 360-degree views of the city and shimmering coastline. Reachable by a short ferry ride, it is a haven for hikers and history buffs, showcasing a raw, volcanic landscape that defines the region’s fiery origins.

Piha beach


Piha Beach is New Zealand’s most famous surf destination, known for its dramatic black iron-sand and powerful Tasman Sea swells. Guarded by the massive Lion Rock, a volcanic monolith splitting the beach, the landscape is rugged and moody. Its treacherous currents make it legendary among surfers, while the surrounding Waitākere Ranges offer lush rainforest trails and waterfalls. It is the quintessential "wild west coast" experience, just a short, winding drive from Auckland.

Bay of Islands


The Bay of Islands is a subtropical micro-region known for its stunning beauty and deep historical significance. Comprising 144 islands dotting turquoise waters, it’s a premier destination for sailing, big-game fishing, and dolphin watching. It is also the "Cradle of New Zealand," home to the Waitangi Treaty Grounds, where the nation’s founding document was signed. With its golden beaches, secluded coves, and the charming seaside town of Russell, it offers a perfect blend of adventure and heritage.

Lake Taupo


Lake Taupō is the massive, sapphire-blue heart of the North Island, sitting inside a caldera created by one of the Earth’s most powerful volcanic eruptions. It is a world-renowned destination for trout fishing, sailing, and skydiving. Just north of the lake, the mighty Huka Falls blasts more than 220,000 liters of water per second over a dramatic ledge. With the snow-capped peaks of Tongariro National Park as a backdrop, Taupō is the ultimate hub for alpine and lakeside adventure.

Tongariro National Park/Taranaki


Tongariro National Park is a volcanic desert of dramatic emerald lakes and active craters, offering a rugged, lunar-like trek across the North Island’s spiritual heart. To the west, Mount Taranaki stands in solitary, near-perfect symmetry, its snow-capped peak rising sharply above lush rainforests and the iconic Pouakai reflection tarns. Together, they represent New Zealand’s raw volcanic power, blending ancient Māori legend with world-class alpine adventure across two of the country's most spectacular, high-altitude landscapes.

Waitamo glowworm caves


The Waitomo Glowworm Caves are a subterranean marvel of limestone cathedrals and silent underground rivers. Guided by descendants of the Māori chiefs who first explored them, visitors drift by boat through the Glowworm Grotto, where thousands of Arachnocampa luminosa create a living galaxy of bioluminescent blue light. This 30-million-year-old labyrinth combines stunning stalactites with a silent, starry atmosphere, offering one of New Zealand’s most magical and culturally significant natural experiences.
